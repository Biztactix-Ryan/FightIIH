Pulsatile Tinnitus in IIH

Understanding the rhythmic whooshing sound

What is Pulsatile Tinnitus?

Pulsatile tinnitus is a rhythmic sound in your ears that beats in time with your pulse. In IIH, it's caused by turbulent blood flow near the ears due to increased intracranial pressure. Unlike regular tinnitus (constant ringing), pulsatile tinnitus has a distinct rhythm.

üéµ Sound Characteristics

  • Whooshing - Like wind or ocean waves
  • Rhythmic - Matches your heartbeat
  • May be one or both ears
  • Volume varies - Soft to very loud
  • Can be constant or intermittent

üîä Common Descriptions

  • "Like a washing machine"
  • "Ocean in my ear"
  • "Heartbeat in my head"
  • "Swooshing sound"
  • "Wind tunnel effect"

‚è∞ When It's Worse

  • Lying down - Especially on affected side
  • Physical activity - Exercise increases it
  • Stress or anxiety
  • High blood pressure moments
  • Quiet environments - More noticeable

üéØ What Helps

  • Head position changes
  • Gentle neck pressure - Sometimes stops it
  • Background noise - Masks the sound
  • IIH treatment - Reducing pressure
  • Stress reduction

Pulsatile vs. Regular Tinnitus

Feature Pulsatile Tinnitus Regular Tinnitus
Sound Pattern Rhythmic, matches pulse Constant ringing/buzzing
Cause Vascular (blood flow) Often nerve-related
Can others hear it? Sometimes (objective) Almost never (subjective)
IIH Connection Very common symptom Less directly related
Investigation Needed Yes - vascular workup Depends on severity

Medical Evaluation

ü©∫ Diagnostic Tests May Include:

  • Physical examination - Doctor may listen with stethoscope
  • MRI/MRA - To check blood vessels
  • CT angiography - Detailed vessel imaging
  • Doppler ultrasound - Blood flow assessment
  • Temporal bone CT - Ear structure evaluation
  • Hearing tests - Rule out hearing loss

Treatment Connection

How IIH Treatment May Help:

  • Pressure reduction - Often improves or eliminates the sound
  • Medications - Acetazolamide may help
  • Weight loss - Can reduce symptoms
  • Lumbar punctures - Temporary relief
  • Shunting - For severe cases
  • Stenting - If venous stenosis present

‚ö†Ô∏è When to Seek Help

  • New onset pulsatile tinnitus - Always needs evaluation
  • Changes in pattern or intensity
  • Associated with other symptoms - Headache, vision changes
  • Hearing loss - Even if mild
  • Dizziness or vertigo

üí° Remember

Pulsatile tinnitus in IIH often improves with treatment of the underlying pressure. While it can be distressing, it's typically not dangerous on its own. Many people find it becomes less noticeable over time as they adapt. Support groups can be helpful for sharing coping strategies.
