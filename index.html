What is Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension?

Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH), formerly known as pseudotumor cerebri, is a neurological condition characterized by increased pressure within the skull (intracranial pressure) without an identifiable cause.

Key Features:

  • Idiopathic: No identifiable underlying cause
  • Intracranial: Occurs within the skull
  • Hypertension: Elevated pressure

Who Does IIH Affect?

IIH primarily affects women of childbearing age, particularly those who are overweight. However, it can occur in anyone, including children and men.

Important Note

IIH can cause permanent vision loss if left untreated. Early diagnosis and treatment are crucial for preserving vision and managing symptoms.

Symptoms of IIH

IIH symptoms are primarily related to increased intracranial pressure and can significantly impact quality of life.

Primary Symptoms

  • Headaches

    Often severe, daily headaches that may worsen with coughing, sneezing, or straining

  • Visual Disturbances

    Blurred vision, double vision, or temporary vision loss

  • Papilledema

    Swelling of the optic nerve visible during eye examination

  • Pulsatile Tinnitus

    Hearing a rhythmic sound that matches your heartbeat

Secondary Symptoms

  • Nausea and Vomiting

    Often accompanying severe headaches

  • Neck and Shoulder Pain

    Tension and stiffness in neck muscles

  • Cognitive Issues

    Memory problems, difficulty concentrating

  • Fatigue

    Persistent tiredness and low energy

Diagnosis of IIH

Diagnosing IIH requires a comprehensive evaluation to rule out other causes of increased intracranial pressure.

Modified Dandy Criteria

The diagnosis of IIH is based on the Modified Dandy Criteria:

  1. Papilledema

    Swelling of the optic disc visible on fundoscopy

  2. Normal neurological examination

    Except for cranial nerve abnormalities

  3. Normal brain imaging

    CT or MRI shows no mass lesion, hydrocephalus, or structural abnormality

  4. Normal CSF composition

    Cerebrospinal fluid analysis shows normal cell count and chemistry

  5. Elevated opening pressure

Diagnostic Tests

Fundoscopy

Eye examination to detect papilledema and monitor optic nerve swelling

Visual Field Testing

Perimetry to assess peripheral vision and detect blind spots

Advanced Brain Imaging

MRI with MR venography to rule out tumors, detect venous sinus stenosis, and identify indirect signs of raised pressure (empty sella, slit ventricles)

Lumbar Puncture

Spinal tap to measure cerebrospinal fluid pressure and composition

OCT Scan

Optical coherence tomography provides quantitative measurement of optic nerve head edema and retinal nerve fiber layer thickness. Essential for confirming papilledema and monitoring treatment response

Blood Tests

To rule out secondary causes of intracranial hypertension

Treatment Options for IIH

Treatment aims to reduce intracranial pressure, preserve vision, and manage symptoms. Approach varies based on severity and individual patient factors.

First-Line Treatments

Weight Loss

5-10% weight reduction can significantly improve symptoms. Sustained weight loss can lead to disease remission in many patients

Evidence Level: Strong

Acetazolamide (Diamox)

Carbonic anhydrase inhibitor that reduces CSF production

Evidence Level: Strong

Bariatric Surgery

For patients with severe obesity, bariatric surgery shows substantial symptom improvement and medication reduction

Evidence Level: Strong

Emerging Medical Therapies

GLP-1 Receptor Agonists

Exenatide shows direct ICP reduction and significant headache improvement. Phase III trials ongoing for first licensed IIH medication

Evidence Level: Phase II Complete

Topiramate (Topamax)

Multi-benefit therapy: reduces ICP, promotes weight loss, and controls headaches. Increasingly used as initial therapy

Evidence Level: Moderate

Furosemide (Lasix)

Loop diuretic used when acetazolamide is not tolerated

Evidence Level: Limited

Surgical Interventions

Optic Nerve Sheath Fenestration

Creates opening in optic nerve sheath to relieve pressure on the nerve

For: Severe visual field defects

CSF Shunting

Lumboperitoneal or ventriculoperitoneal shunt to divert excess CSF

For: Refractory headaches

Venous Sinus Stenting

Minimally invasive stent placement in stenotic venous sinuses. 94% improvement in papilledema, 80% headache improvement, superior safety profile to shunts

For: Venous pressure gradient >8-10 mmHg

Treatment Algorithm

Step 1: Lifestyle Modifications

Weight loss, dietary changes, regular exercise

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about IIH diagnosis, treatment, and living with the condition.

Diagnosis & Symptoms

How long does it take to get diagnosed with IIH?

Diagnosis can take weeks to months. Many patients see multiple doctors before getting the correct diagnosis. The key is finding a neurologist or neuro-ophthalmologist familiar with IIH who can perform the necessary tests including fundoscopy, brain imaging, and lumbar puncture.

Can IIH be misdiagnosed as migraines?

Yes, IIH headaches are often initially misdiagnosed as migraines or tension headaches. Key differences include daily headaches that worsen with coughing/straining, visual symptoms, and pulsatile tinnitus. If you have persistent daily headaches with these features, request an eye exam to check for papilledema.

What should I do if my vision is getting worse?

Seek immediate medical attention. Vision loss in IIH can be permanent if not treated promptly. Contact your neurologist or go to the emergency room. Don't wait for your next scheduled appointment if you notice new visual symptoms or worsening of existing ones.

Can you have IIH without papilledema?

Yes, this is called "IIH without papilledema" (IIHWOP). Diagnosis requires elevated CSF pressure (‚â•250 mmH‚ÇÇO) plus at least one of: sixth nerve palsy, visual field defect, or neuroimaging signs of elevated ICP. Some patients may have very early disease before papilledema develops.

Treatment & Medication

How much weight do I need to lose to see improvement?

Studies show that 5-10% weight loss can significantly improve symptoms. Even modest weight loss of 10-15 pounds can make a difference. The key is sustained weight loss - yo-yo dieting may worsen symptoms. Work with a nutritionist familiar with IIH if possible.

What are the side effects of acetazolamide (Diamox)?

Common side effects include tingling in hands/feet, metallic taste, kidney stones, fatigue, and loss of appetite. Most side effects improve over time. Stay well-hydrated and consider potassium supplements if recommended by your doctor. Report severe side effects immediately.

When should surgery be considered?

Surgery is considered when: medical treatment fails to control symptoms, vision continues to deteriorate despite treatment, or severe intractable headaches significantly impact quality of life. The choice between different surgical options depends on your specific symptoms and anatomy.

Are there any medications I should avoid?

Avoid vitamin A supplements, tetracycline antibiotics, and growth hormone unless absolutely necessary. Birth control pills and hormone replacement therapy may worsen symptoms in some patients. Always inform doctors about your IIH diagnosis before starting new medications.

Living with IIH

Can I exercise with IIH?

Yes, regular exercise is beneficial and often essential for weight management. Avoid activities that significantly increase intracranial pressure like heavy weightlifting, inverted yoga poses, or activities involving straining. Swimming, walking, and moderate cardio are generally safe and helpful.

Will IIH affect my pregnancy?

IIH can complicate pregnancy. Some women improve during pregnancy, others worsen. Close monitoring by both neurology and high-risk obstetrics is essential. Acetazolamide is generally avoided in pregnancy. Discuss family planning with your medical team before conceiving.

Can IIH go into remission?

Yes, many patients achieve remission, especially with significant weight loss. Studies show sustained remission rates of 50-70% with successful weight management. However, symptoms can recur with weight gain or other triggers, so ongoing monitoring is important even in remission.

How often do I need eye exams?

Initially every 2-4 weeks until stable, then every 3-6 months. OCT scans should be done at each visit to quantitatively monitor optic nerve swelling. More frequent monitoring is needed if symptoms worsen or treatment changes. Visual field testing should be done every 6-12 months.
